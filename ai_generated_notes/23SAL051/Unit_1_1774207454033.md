# Unit 1: Introduction to Artificial Neural Networks

## 1. Biological Neurons: The Foundation of Artificial Intelligence

### Detailed Definition
Biological neurons are the fundamental building blocks of the nervous system in living organisms, including the human brain. They are specialized cells that transmit electrical and chemical signals, forming complex networks responsible for processing information, controlling bodily functions, and enabling cognitive abilities such as learning, memory, and perception. The study of their structure and function has profoundly inspired the design and development of Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), serving as the initial conceptual model for artificial intelligence systems.

### Deep Technical Explanation
A typical biological neuron comprises several key components, each with a distinct function in signal processing:

1.  **Dendrites:** These are tree-like branching extensions that receive incoming electrochemical signals (neurotransmitters) from other neurons. They act as the primary input receptors for the neuron.
2.  **Soma (Cell Body):** The central part of the neuron, containing the nucleus. The soma integrates all the incoming signals received by the dendrites. If the sum of these excitatory and inhibitory inputs reaches a certain threshold potential, it triggers an action potential.
3.  **Axon:** A long, slender projection that extends from the soma. The axon transmits the electrical impulse (action potential) away from the cell body to other neurons, muscles, or glands. It can be myelinated (covered in a fatty sheath) to speed up signal conduction.
4.  **Axon Terminals (Synaptic Terminals):** These are the distal ends of the axon, which form specialized junctions called synapses with the dendrites or cell bodies of other neurons.
5.  **Synapse:** The functional junction between two neurons where information is transmitted. When an action potential reaches the axon terminal, it triggers the release of neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft, which then bind to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, influencing its electrical potential.

The functioning of a biological neuron involves an **electrochemical process**. When excitatory signals accumulate and depolarize the neuron's membrane potential past a critical **threshold**, an **action potential** (a brief, rapid reversal of membrane potential) is generated and propagated down the axon in an "all-or-none" fashion. This means the action potential either fires completely or not at all; its amplitude is consistent, though its *frequency* can vary depending on the strength of the input.

A crucial aspect of biological neural networks is **synaptic plasticity**, which refers to the ability of synapses to strengthen or weaken over time in response to activity. This dynamic modification of synaptic efficacy is widely regarded as the cellular basis for learning and memory. When two neurons repeatedly communicate, the connection between them can become stronger (long-term potentiation), or weaker (long-term depression), effectively "learning" patterns from experiences.

### Secondary Sub-topics: Biological vs. Artificial Neurons
While biological neurons serve as inspiration, it's critical to understand that Artificial Neural Networks are **highly abstract computational models**, not direct simulations of the brain. Key differences include:
*   **Complexity:** Biological neurons are vastly more complex, involving thousands of biochemical processes, complex spatio-temporal dynamics, and diverse neurotransmitter systems. ANNs simplify this to a weighted sum and an activation function.
*   **Processing Speed:** Biological neurons operate in milliseconds, but in parallel. Artificial neurons compute much faster on electronic hardware.
*   **Learning Mechanism:** Biological learning (e.g., Hebbian learning, STDP) is local and unsupervised. ANN learning often involves global error propagation (backpropagation) and large datasets.
*   **Structure:** Biological networks are highly recurrent and irregular. ANNs are often structured in distinct layers, predominantly feedforward.

### Logical Derivations (Inspiration for ANN Design)
The abstract model of an artificial neuron directly stems from observations of biological neurons:
*   **Inputs:** Dendrites receiving multiple signals correspond to the input vector $X = [x_1, x_2, ..., x_n]$.
*   **Synaptic Weights:** The varying strength of synaptic connections is modeled by learnable weights $W = [w_1, w_2, ..., w_n]$. A stronger synapse translates to a larger weight.
*   **Summation/Integration:** The soma integrating signals is represented by the weighted sum $\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i$.
*   **Thresholding/Activation:** The "all-or-none" action potential firing if a threshold is crossed is modeled by an activation function $f(\cdot)$ that transforms the integrated sum into an output signal.

## 2. The Perceptron: The Simplest Artificial Neuron

### Detailed Definition
The Perceptron, introduced by Frank Rosenblatt in 1957, is the simplest form of an Artificial Neural Network, representing a single artificial neuron. It is a linear binary classifier, designed to make decisions or classify input data into one of two categories. It was a pioneering algorithm that could learn from data, adjusting its internal parameters (weights) to correctly classify input patterns, thus marking a significant milestone in the history of AI and machine learning.

### Deep Technical Explanation
A Perceptron consists of four main components:

1.  **Inputs ($x_i$):** These are the features or data points fed into the neuron.
2.  **Weights ($w_i$):** Each input $x_i$ is associated with a corresponding weight $w_i$. These weights represent the strength or importance of each input to the neuron's decision.
3.  **Bias ($b$):** An additional input, often considered as a weight for an input that is always 1. The bias term allows the decision boundary to be shifted independently of the input features, providing greater flexibility in classification.
4.  **Summation Function:** The inputs are multiplied by their respective weights, and these products are summed up along with the bias term. This weighted sum is often called the *net input* or *activation potential* ($z$).
    $$ z = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i + b $$
5.  **Activation Function:** The net input $z$ is then passed through an activation function, typically a **step function** (also known as a Heaviside step function or threshold function) for a classical Perceptron. This function determines the output of the neuron.
    $$ \text{output } y = f(z) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } z \ge \text{threshold} \\ 0 & \text{if } z < \text{threshold} \end{cases} $$
    For simplicity, the threshold is often absorbed into the bias term, so we compare $z$ to 0.

The Perceptron's goal is to learn a set of weights and a bias such that it can correctly classify new, unseen data. This learning occurs through the **Perceptron Learning Rule**:

*   **Initialization:** Weights $w_i$ and bias $b$ are typically initialized to small random values or zeros.
*   **Iteration:** For each training example $(X, \text{target\_output})$:
    1.  **Calculate Output:** Compute the Perceptron's output $y$ using the current weights and bias.
    2.  **Compare and Update:** If the output $y$ does not match the target output $t$:
        *   Weights are updated as: $w_{i, \text{new}} = w_{i, \text{old}} + \alpha (t - y) x_i$
        *   Bias is updated as: $b_{\text{new}} = b_{\text{old}} + \alpha (t - y)$
        Where $\alpha$ (alpha) is the **learning rate**, a small positive constant that controls the step size of the weight updates. The term $(t - y)$ represents the error. If $y=t$, no update occurs. If $y=0, t=1$ (output is too low), weights are increased. If $y=1, t=0$ (output is too high), weights are decreased.
*   **Convergence:** The process iterates over the training data until all training examples are correctly classified (or for a fixed number of epochs). The **Perceptron Convergence Theorem** states that if the training data is **linearly separable**, the Perceptron learning algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a solution in a finite number of steps.

### Secondary Sub-topics: Limitations and Significance
The Perceptron's primary limitation became evident with Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert's 1969 book "Perceptrons," which highlighted its inability to solve problems that are not linearly separable, most famously the **XOR (exclusive OR) problem**. For example, a single Perceptron cannot classify points (0,0) and (1,1) as one class and (0,1) and (1,0) as another, because no single straight line can separate these groups.

Despite this limitation, the Perceptron was hugely significant as it demonstrated that a simple computational model could learn from data and adapt its internal parameters, laying the groundwork for more complex multi-layer neural networks that overcome these limitations. It introduced the core concepts of weighted inputs, summation, thresholding, and an iterative learning rule.

### Logical Derivations (Weight Update Rule)
The weight update rule can be intuitively derived from a desire to reduce classification error. If the Perceptron outputs $y$ and the target is $t$:
*   If $y < t$ (e.g., $y=0, t=1$), the net input $z$ was too low. We need to increase $z$. To do this, we should increase weights $w_i$ for positive inputs $x_i$ and decrease them for negative $x_i$. This is achieved by adding $\alpha \cdot (1-0) \cdot x_i = \alpha x_i$ to $w_i$.
*   If $y > t$ (e.g., $y=1, t=0$), the net input $z$ was too high. We need to decrease $z$. To do this, we should decrease weights $w_i$ for positive inputs $x_i$ and increase them for negative $x_i$. This is achieved by adding $\alpha \cdot (0-1) \cdot x_i = -\alpha x_i$ to $w_i$.
Combining these, the update rule $w_{i, \text{new}} = w_{i, \text{old}} + \alpha (t - y) x_i$ consistently adjusts weights in the direction that reduces the error. The bias update $b_{\text{new}} = b_{\text{old}} + \alpha (t - y)$ works similarly, shifting the decision boundary.

### Real-world Engineering Instances
While a single Perceptron's direct applications are limited due to its linear separability constraint, it's foundational:
*   **Simple Logic Gates:** A Perceptron can easily implement basic logic gates like AND, OR, and NOT, which are linearly separable. For example, an AND gate with inputs $x_1, x_2$, weights $w_1=0.5, w_2=0.5$, and bias $b=-0.7$ (and threshold 0) correctly classifies (1,1) as 1 and all others as 0.
*   **Basic Spam Filtering:** Early, very crude spam filters could use Perceptrons to classify emails as spam or not spam based on a few keyword counts (e.g., if "Viagra" count > threshold, classify as spam). This is a linear decision boundary.
*   **Early Optical Character Recognition (OCR):** Simple Perceptrons were used in early OCR systems to distinguish between two distinct patterns (e.g., differentiating between 'A' and 'B' if they had clearly distinct features that allowed for linear separation in the feature space).

## 3. Activation Functions: Introducing Non-linearity

### Detailed Definition
Activation functions are critical non-linear transformations applied to the weighted sum of inputs in a neural network neuron. Their primary role is to introduce non-linearity into the model, enabling the network to learn complex patterns and represent non-linear relationships between inputs and outputs. Without non-linear activation functions, a multi-layer neural network would simply degenerate into a single-layer linear model, regardless of the number of hidden layers, severely limiting its representational power.

### Deep Technical Explanation
The output of a neuron is computed as $y = f(\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i + b)$, where $f(\cdot)$ is the activation function. The necessity of non-linearity arises from the fact that many real-world datasets exhibit non-linear patterns. If all activation functions were linear, then any combination of layers would still produce a linear transformation of the input, effectively making the entire deep network behave like a single Perceptron. Non-linear activation functions allow neural networks to approximate arbitrary complex functions, a property often referred to as the Universal Approximation Theorem for sufficiently wide and deep networks.

Key properties desired in activation functions for training deep neural networks include:
*   **Non-linearity:** Essential for learning complex mappings.
*   **Differentiability:** Crucial for gradient-based optimization algorithms like backpropagation, which rely on computing the derivative of the loss function with respect to the network's weights.
*   **Monotonicity:** Generally preferred, meaning the function is either non-decreasing or non-increasing.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Should be fast to compute during forward and backward passes.
*   **Avoidance of Vanishing/Exploding Gradients:** Ideal functions help maintain gradients within a reasonable range during training.

We will delve into three common activation functions:

#### 3.1. Sigmoid Function (Logistic Function)
*   **Definition:** The Sigmoid function, denoted as $\sigma(x)$, maps any real-valued input into the range $(0, 1)$.
    $$ \sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}} $$
*   **Properties:**
    *   **Smooth and Differentiable:** This property makes it suitable for gradient-based learning.
    *   **Output Range (0, 1):** Historically used for output layers where probabilities are desired, as it squashes values between 0 and 1.
*   **Gradient Derivation:**
    $$ \frac{d\sigma}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx} (1 + e^{-x})^{-1} $$
    $$ = -1 (1 + e^{-x})^{-2} (-e^{-x}) $$
    $$ = \frac{e^{-x}}{(1 + e^{-x})^2} $$
    This can be expressed in terms of $\sigma(x)$ itself:
    $$ \frac{e^{-x}}{(1 + e^{-x})^2} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}} \cdot \frac{e^{-x}}{1 + e^{-x}} = \sigma(x) \cdot \frac{e^{-x}}{1 + e^{-x}} = \sigma(x) \cdot (1 - \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}) $$
    $$ \therefore \frac{d\sigma}{dx} = \sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x)) $$
*   **Drawbacks:**
    *   **Vanishing Gradients:** For very large positive or negative inputs, the sigmoid function saturates, meaning its gradient becomes extremely close to zero. During backpropagation, these small gradients get multiplied across layers, leading to exponentially small gradients in earlier layers. This makes learning very slow or stops it entirely.
    *   **Output Not Zero-Centered:** The outputs are always positive (between 0 and 1). This can lead to issues during gradient descent. If all inputs to a subsequent layer are positive, the gradients for the weights in that layer will either all be positive or all negative, leading to "zig-zagging" optimization paths rather than directly towards the optimum.

#### 3.2. Hyperbolic Tangent Function (Tanh)
*   **Definition:** The Tanh function is a rescaled version of the sigmoid function, mapping any real-valued input into the range $(-1, 1)$.
    $$ \tanh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}} $$
    It can also be expressed in terms of the sigmoid function: $\tanh(x) = 2\sigma(2x) - 1$.
*   **Properties:**
    *   **Smooth and Differentiable:** Similar to sigmoid.
    *   **Output Range (-1, 1):** Its output is zero-centered, which is generally preferred over sigmoid as it helps alleviate the "zig-zagging" gradient problem and can make optimization easier.
*   **Gradient Derivation:**
    $$ \frac{d}{dx} \tanh(x) = \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}} \right) $$
    Using the quotient rule $(u/v)' = (u'v - uv')/v^2$:
    $u = e^x - e^{-x} \implies u' = e^x + e^{-x}$
    $v = e^x + e^{-x} \implies v' = e^x - e^{-x}$
    $$ = \frac{(e^x + e^{-x})(e^x + e^{-x}) - (e^x - e^{-x})(e^x - e^{-x})}{(e^x + e^{-x})^2} $$
    $$ = \frac{(e^x + e^{-x})^2 - (e^x - e^{-x})^2}{(e^x + e^{-x})^2} $$
    $$ = 1 - \left( \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}} \right)^2 $$
    $$ \therefore \frac{d}{dx} \tanh(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x) $$
*   **Drawbacks:**
    *   **Vanishing Gradients:** While zero-centered, Tanh still suffers from the vanishing gradient problem for very large positive or negative inputs, just like sigmoid, because its gradient also approaches zero in those regions.

#### 3.3. Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU)
*   **Definition:** The Rectified Linear Unit, or ReLU, is the most widely used activation function in deep learning today. It outputs the input directly if it is positive, otherwise, it outputs zero.
    $$ ReLU(x) = \max(0, x) = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x > 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } x \le 0 \end{cases} $$
*   **Properties:**
    *   **Non-saturating for Positive Inputs:** For $x > 0$, the gradient is 1, which helps to mitigate the vanishing gradient problem significantly. This allows networks to learn faster and avoid saturation in the positive region.
    *   **Computational Efficiency:** Simple to implement and compute, involving only a comparison and assignment.
    *   **Sparse Activation:** For negative inputs, the output is 0, leading to "sparse" activations (many neurons output 0), which can be computationally efficient.
*   **Gradient Derivation:**
    $$ \frac{d}{dx} ReLU(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x > 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } x < 0 \\ \text{undefined} & \text{if } x = 0 \end{cases} $$
    In practice, for $x=0$, the subgradient can be any value between 0 and 1, but it's typically set to 0 or 1.
*   **Drawbacks:**
    *   **Dying ReLU Problem:** If a large negative bias or strong negative weights cause the weighted sum $z$ for a ReLU neuron to always be negative, the neuron will always output 0. Consequently, its gradient will always be 0, and it will stop learning (i.e., its weights will not be updated through backpropagation). This neuron effectively "dies" and contributes nothing to the network.
    *   **Output Not Zero-Centered:** Similar to sigmoid, the output is always non-negative.

### Secondary Sub-topics: ReLU Variants
To address the "dying ReLU" problem, several variants have been proposed:
*   **Leaky ReLU:** $f(x) = \max(\alpha x, x)$, where $\alpha$ is a small positive constant (e.g., 0.01). It allows a small, non-zero gradient for negative inputs, preventing neurons from dying.
*   **Parametric ReLU (PReLU):** Similar to Leaky ReLU, but $\alpha$ is a learnable parameter rather than a fixed constant.
*   **Exponential Linear Unit (ELU):** $f(x) = x$ for $x > 0$ and $f(x) = \alpha (e^x - 1)$ for $x \le 0$. It also produces negative outputs, pushing the mean activation towards zero, and has a smoother curve for negative inputs.

### Logical Derivations
The gradients are fundamental for understanding how weights are updated during the backpropagation algorithm. For instance, the sigmoid's gradient $\sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x))$ peaks at $x=0$ (where $\sigma(x)=0.5$) with a value of $0.25$, and quickly drops towards zero as $|x|$ increases. This mathematical behavior directly explains the vanishing gradient issue. Similarly, ReLU's constant gradient of 1 for $x>0$ mathematically explains its effectiveness in mitigating vanishing gradients in that region.

### Real-world Engineering Instances
*   **Sigmoid/Tanh:** Historically used in the hidden layers of Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) for tasks like hand-written digit recognition (e.g., LeNet architecture). Sigmoid is still commonly used in the output layer of binary classification problems to interpret output as a probability.
*   **ReLU:** Dominant in hidden layers of almost all modern deep learning architectures, including Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for image recognition (e.g., ResNet, VGG) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) for natural language processing (though GRU/LSTM are more common). Its efficiency and vanishing gradient mitigation are key.

## 4. Neural Network Architecture: From Perceptron to Multi-Layer Networks

### Detailed Definition
Neural Network Architecture refers to the specific arrangement and configuration of artificial neurons (nodes) into layers, and how these layers are interconnected to form a computational model. It dictates the flow of information, the complexity of patterns the network can learn, and its overall computational capacity. While a single Perceptron is the simplest architecture, the power of neural networks truly emerges when multiple neurons are organized into layers, forming Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) or deeper architectures.

### Deep Technical Explanation
The architecture of a typical feedforward neural network, which is the foundational structure, consists of several layers:

1.  **Input Layer:**
    *   **Function:** This layer receives the raw input features of the dataset. It does not perform any computation (no weights, no activation function). Its nodes simply pass the input values to the next layer.
    *   **Structure:** The number of nodes in the input layer is determined by the number of features in each input sample. For example, if an image has 784 pixels (28x28), the input layer would have 784 nodes.

2.  **Hidden Layers:**
    *   **Function:** These layers perform the bulk of the computation within the network. Each node in a hidden layer receives inputs from the previous layer, applies a set of weights, sums them up with a bias, and then passes the result through an activation function. The outputs of these nodes become inputs to the next layer. Hidden layers are responsible for learning increasingly abstract and complex representations of the input data.
    *   **Structure:** A network can have one or multiple hidden layers. Networks with more than one hidden layer are considered "deep" neural networks. The number of nodes (width) in each hidden layer is a hyperparameter chosen by the designer, often determined through experimentation or heuristic rules. Hidden layers introduce the non-linearity crucial for solving complex problems.
    *   **Connections:** In a fully connected (Dense) layer, every neuron in the hidden layer is connected to every neuron in the preceding layer.

3.  **Output Layer:**
    *   **Function:** This layer produces the final predictions or outputs of the network. The type of activation function and the number of nodes in this layer depend entirely on the nature of the task.
    *   **Structure:**
        *   **Regression (predicting a continuous value):** Typically one node with a linear (identity) activation function, or ReLU if the output must be non-negative.
        *   **Binary Classification (two classes):** One node with a Sigmoid activation function, outputting a probability between 0 and 1.
        *   **Multi-class Classification (N > 2 classes):** N nodes, one for each class, often with a Softmax activation function (which outputs a probability distribution over the N classes, summing to 1).

**Information Flow (Feedforward Networks):**
In a feedforward neural network, information flows in only one direction, from the input layer, through any hidden layers, to the output layer. There are no cycles or loops. This unidirectional flow simplifies the learning process.

**Network Depth vs. Width:**
*   **Depth:** Refers to the number of hidden layers. Deeper networks (with more hidden layers) can learn more abstract and hierarchical representations, often leading to better performance on complex tasks.
*   **Width:** Refers to the number of neurons in each hidden layer. Wider layers can capture more distinct features at a given level of abstraction.

**Bias Term Revisited:**
The bias term ($b$) plays a crucial role in each neuron. It allows the activation function to be shifted along the input axis. Without a bias, the activation function would always pass through the origin $(0,0)$, limiting the network's ability to fit data. For example, if a neuron only uses ReLU, and all its weights are positive, it can only activate for positive weighted sums. A bias term can make it activate even if the weighted sum is zero, by adding a constant offset.

**Mathematical Representation (Forward Propagation):**
For a layer $l$ in a neural network (where $l=0$ is the input layer), let $a^{(l-1)}$ be the vector of activations from the previous layer, $W^{(l)}$ be the weight matrix connecting layer $l-1$ to layer $l$, and $b^{(l)}$ be the bias vector for layer $l$.
The raw input to the neurons in layer $l$ (before activation) is often denoted as $z^{(l)}$:
$$ z^{(l)} = W^{(l)} a^{(l-1)} + b^{(l)} $$
The activation output of layer $l$, $a^{(l)}$, is then obtained by applying the activation function $f^{(l)}(\cdot)$ element-wise to $z^{(l)}$:
$$ a^{(l)} = f^{(l)}(z^{(l)}) $$
This process is repeated for each layer until the output layer is reached.

### Secondary Sub-topics: Fully Connected Layers
The architecture described above, where every neuron in one layer is connected to every neuron in the next layer, is known as a **Fully Connected Layer** or a **Dense Layer**. A network composed entirely of such layers (plus activation functions) is a **Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)**. While MLPs are powerful universal approximators, they are not always the most efficient architectures for certain data types (e.g., images, sequences) due to their large number of parameters.

### Logical Derivations (Forward Propagation for a Simple MLP)
Consider a simple 2-layer MLP (1 hidden layer, 1 output layer) with $N_0$ input features, $N_1$ neurons in the hidden layer, and $N_2$ neurons in the output layer.

1.  **Input Layer to Hidden Layer:**
    *   Input vector: $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N_0}$
    *   Weight matrix for hidden layer: $W^{(1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_1 \times N_0}$
    *   Bias vector for hidden layer: $b^{(1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_1}$
    *   Net input to hidden layer: $Z^{(1)} = W^{(1)} X + b^{(1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_1}$
    *   Activations of hidden layer: $A^{(1)} = f^{(1)}(Z^{(1)}) \in \mathbb{R}^{N_1}$ (where $f^{(1)}$ is the activation function for the hidden layer, e.g., ReLU or Tanh).

2.  **Hidden Layer to Output Layer:**
    *   Input (from hidden layer): $A^{(1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_1}$
    *   Weight matrix for output layer: $W^{(2)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_2 \times N_1}$
    *   Bias vector for output layer: $b^{(2)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_2}$
    *   Net input to output layer: $Z^{(2)} = W^{(2)} A^{(1)} + b^{(2)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_2}$
    *   Activations of output layer (final prediction): $A^{(2)} = f^{(2)}(Z^{(2)}) \in \mathbb{R}^{N_2}$ (where $f^{(2)}$ is the activation function for the output layer, e.g., Sigmoid for binary, Softmax for multi-class).

This sequence of matrix multiplications and element-wise activation functions constitutes the **forward propagation** pass, which is how the network computes an output given an input.

### Real-world Engineering Instances
*   **MLPs for Tabular Data:** Multi-Layer Perceptrons are widely used for tasks involving structured (tabular) data, such as customer churn prediction, credit scoring, fraud detection, and general classification or regression problems where features are clearly defined. For example, predicting house prices based on features like area, number of rooms, and location.
*   **Early Image Recognition:** While CNNs are dominant now, early attempts at image recognition (e.g., for simple digit recognition like MNIST) often utilized MLPs, where each pixel was treated as an input feature.
*   **Pattern Recognition:** MLPs can learn to recognize complex patterns in various domains, from medical diagnostics based on patient records to industrial quality control based on sensor readings.

### Common Exam Pitfalls/Assessor's Note
Students often confuse the roles of input, hidden, and output layers, or fail to understand the fully connected nature of MLPs. A common mistake is not grasping the critical role of non-linear activation functions in hidden layers, thinking that more linear layers would increase capacity. Inability to write down the forward propagation equations using matrix notation for a simple network is a significant pitfall. Emphasis should be placed on the architectural components, their function, and the data flow, as well as the distinction between network width and depth.
